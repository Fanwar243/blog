Song Interpretation: Neighbourhood #1 (Tunnels) by Arcade Fire

And if the snow buries my
My neighbourhood
And if my parents are crying
Then I'll dig a tunnel
From my window to yours
Yeah, a tunnel from my window to yours

The author begins the first line with “And,” subtly implying that we are being dropped into the story in medias res, and the song does not capture the character’s whole journey. The character describes a metaphorical snow engulfing their neighbourhood, both elements being crucial to the story. The snow, colourless and pure, symbolises a masking of the character’s neighbourhood (a symbol representing their childhood home and family) with something bland and emotionless, a cold outer exterior imposed on what they want their life to be. From the line “And if my parents are crying,” we are given a glimpse into the character’s troubled life at home. Their parents could be quarrelling with each other, either about their own relationship, or perhaps they are crying because of the author. The way the line is written alludes to the fact that it is a regular occurence in their house. As such, as explained by the lines that follow, the author is looking for a way out. They sing of digging a tunnel from their window to that of another character, who perhaps is their closest friend or lover. The absurdity of this solution signals the character’s desperation for escape.

You climb out of the chimney
And meet me in the middle
The middle of the town
And since there's no one else around
We let our hair grow long and forget all we used to know
Then our skin gets thicker from living out in the snow

This stanza describes the two characters sneaking out of their homes and meeting somewhere “where there is no else around.” The line “the middle of the town” tells us that this does not mean they are literally alone, but that they are somewhere where there is no one to judge them. They let “their hair grow long,” symbolising their ability to live the way they want to and be their true selves without the disapproval of their family. Their skin has gotten thicker “from living out in the snow,” meaning that repressing their inner identities from their families for so long has made them tougher, defiant of judgment from other people.

You change all the lead
Sleeping in my head
As the day grows dim
I hear you sing a golden hymn

This stanza is the first to allude to the theme of alchemy in the song. Alchemy is the mythical practice of turning lead into gold, which can be interpreted here as changing something cold and colourless (lead, but can also be snow), into something bright and wonderful (gold). As the author wallows in depression, they hear their lover sing the song of alchemy (the golden hymn). Being with their special person turns all the heavy thoughts in their head to something more hopeful and happy.

Then we tried to name our babies
But we forgot all the names that
The names we used to know

The author describes a (potentially hypothetical) situation of raising a family with their lover. The significance of forgetting the "names we used to know" is that the both of them have changed so much in their lives and have moved on to a stage where the people in their childhood are only distant memories. Another, more powerful, significance of these lines is that the two are raising a family of their own, but not naming their babies with the "names they used to know" symbolises that they will not treat their children the same way they were treated in their childhood.

But sometimes we remember our bedrooms
And our parent's bedrooms
And the bedrooms of our friends
Then we think of our parents
Well what ever happened to them?

Sometimes, the author's mind wanders to their childhood home. They start thinking of their past experiences, what they went through with their friends and family, all the bitter and the sweet. Those hazy thoughts finally focus on their parents, who, as the writing of the lyrics imply, have long since left the author's life.

You change all the lead
Sleeping in my head to gold as the day grows dim
I hear you sing a golden hymn
The song I've been trying to sing

Here the song of alchemy is sung again, with the addition of a new line. From the line “The song I’ve been trying to sing,” the author wishes to convey that they not only find solace in their lover, but that they want to become more like them. It is a declaration of admiration, of love, because the sign of a deep love is the yearning to become the person we love. Above trying to become close to them, we try to imitate them. The author sees in their lover their ability in turning the author’s negative thoughts into happier ones, and wants to do the same for them.

Purify the colors, purify my mind
Purify the colors, purify my mind
And spread the ashes of the colors
Over this heart of mine

The first two lines are also references to alchemy, where the purification of matter (the colors) is a metaphor for the purification of the mind. The colours here harken back to the earlier metaphor of the snow. The introduction of colours symbolises the washing away of the colourless facade (snow) over the author’s inner self. At the euphoric conclusion of the song, the author’s story is not yet ended, but it has transitioned to a new period. They are finally free of the haunting thoughts of a repressive childhood home, of family and friends that have long since left their lives, and of their previous, false outer self. It is more than an absolution of the guilt they feel over cutting ties with their family, it is an absolution of their former self.
